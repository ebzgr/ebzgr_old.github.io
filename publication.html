A Recursive Partitioning Approach for Dynamic Discrete Choice Modeling in High Dimensional Settings

Joint work with H. Yoganarasimhan
Working PaperWorking paper

Abstract

Dynamic discrete choice models are widely employed to answer substantive and policy questions in settings where individuals' current choices have future implications. However, estimation of these models is often computationally intensive and/or infeasible in high-dimensional settings. Indeed, even specifying the structure for how the utilities/state transitions enter the agent's decision is challenging in high dimensional settings when we have no guiding theory. In this paper, we present a semi-parametric formulation of dynamic discrete choice models that incorporates a high-dimensional set of state variables, in addition to the standard variables used in a parametric utility function. The high-dimensional variable can include all the variables that are not the main variables of interest, but may potentially affect people choices and must be included in estimation procedure, i.e., nuisance variables. We present a data-driven recursive partitioning algorithm that can reduce the dimensionality of the high-dimensional state space such that choices and state transitions within a partition behave similarly. Our machine learning approach can reduce estimation bias and make estimation feasible at the same time. We present Monte Carlo simulations to demonstrate the performance of our method compared to standard estimation methods.

Design and Evaluation of Optimal Free Trials

Joint work with H. Yoganarasimhan and A. Pani.
Journal PaperUnder second round of revision at Management Science

Abstract

Free trial promotions are a commonly used customer acquisition strategy in the Software as a Service (SaaS) industry. We use data from a large-scale field experiment conducted by a leading SaaS firm to study the effect of trial length on outcomes and the returns from personalized trial length assignment. We find that the 7-days trial is the best average treatment that maximizes customer acquisition, retention, and profitability. In terms of mechanism, we rule out the demand cannibalization theory, find support for the consumer learning hypothesis, and show that long stretches of inactivity at the end of the trial are associated with lower conversions. We then develop a framework for personalized targeting policy design and evaluation. We first learn a lasso model of outcomes as a function of users' pre-treatment variables and treatment. Next, we use individual-level predictions of the outcome to assign the optimal treatment to each user. We then evaluate the personalized policy using the inverse propensity score reward estimator. We find that a personalization based on lasso leads to 6.8\% improvement in subscription compared to a uniform 30-days for all policy. It also performs well on long-term customer retention and revenues. Segmentation analysis suggests that skilled and experienced users are more likely to benefit from longer trials. Finally, we see that personalized policies based on many outcome estimators and heterogeneous treatment effects estimators (e.g., generalized random forests) perform poorly. This suggests that adopting a simple uniform policy can be better than personalizing policies based on these methods.

Empathizing and Systemizing Skills Influence Risky Decision Making in Children

Joint work with A.H. Memari, M. Shayestefar, S. Gharibzadeh, S. Hafizi and P.Moshayedi
Journal Papers Appeared at the Learning and Individual Differences Volume 40 (May 2015)

Abstract

Tow-mode theories explain that risky behaviors in young individuals may be the result of competition between an affective system (e.g., Empathizing system) and a deliberative system (e.g., Systemizing system). We aimed to investigate a total of 134 children aged 7–12 years old on scores of risky decision making and empathizing and systemizing measures. The primary analysis indicated that systemizers showed higher sensitivity to punishment than those in balanced or empathizer classes (F (2,130) = 14.8, p < 0.001). Furthermore empathizers indicated higher level of risk taking than balanced and empathizer children (F (2,130) = 5.027, p = 0.005). Providing further support for two-mode theories in risky decision making, findings suggest that empathizing and systemizing measures may be useful indexes for addressing affective and deliberative systems.

Some Bounds for the Signed Edge Domination Number of a Graph

Joint work with S. Akbari, H. Esfandiari and S. Seddighin
Journal Paper Appeared at the Australasian Journal of Combinatorics Volume58(1)(2014).

Abstract

The closed neighbourhood NG[e] of an edge e in a graph G is the set consisting of e and of all edges having a common end vertex with e. Let f be a function on the edges of G into the set {−1, 1}. If Σ e∈NG[x] f(e) ≥ 1 for every x ∈ E(G), then f is called a signed edge domination function of G. The minimum value ofΣx∈E(G) f(x), taken over every signed edge domination function f of G, is called signed edge domination number of G and denoted by γ's(G). It has been proved that γ's(G) ≥ n − m for every graph G of order n and size m. In this paper we prove that γ's(G) ≥ (2α'(G)−m)/3 for every simple graph G, where α'(G) is the size of a maximum matching of G. We also prove that for a simple graph G of order n whose each vertex has an odd degree, γ's(G) ≤ n − 2α'(G)/3.

Evolution of Empathic Altruism: An Evolutionary Model

Joint work with A.H. Memari
Conference Poster Appeared in Basic and Clinical Neuroscience Congress

Abstract

Altruistic behaviour is commonplace in every biological organization, from genes to multi-cellular organism. Nevertheless, it is still debating how such costly behaviour endures in strict competition for survival? Kin selection, Group selection and Reciprocal altruism are provided explanations by Darwinian evolution theory. Many models has been proposed for the evolution of altruism through these mechanisms, especially reciprocal altruism. Most of them, however, cannot interpret human altruism toward a complete stranger where there is not any social evaluation or reciprocation (e.g. People's donation to distant tsunami victims). Affective empathy is proposed to be the motivation behind such behaviours. In this study, we aimed to illustrate how affective altruism could survive, utilizing a computational evolutionary model.

A Computational Model on Coevolution of Cooperation and Response to Inequity

Under the Supervision of Dr. J. Habibi
Thesis Bachelor thesis

Abstract

Although at first sight into Darwinian evolution theory, it seems that cooperation is in contrast with Natural Selection, empirical data and experiments confirm the existence of such behavior in a variety of animals. Why should an individual help another, while he is in competition for survival with whom? Yet, this behavior can be seen among bacteria, viruses, trees, animals and human.

In the recent years, for investigating this behavior, useful means have been utilized by scientists. Meanwhile, Game Theory and Evolutionary Game Theory models have been really useful. Social games are simplified models of human social behavior that are employed by scientists. Different mechanisms have been proposed for Evolution of Cooperation based on these Social games and Game Theory.

On the other hand, recent evidence demonstrates that humans are not the only species to respond negatively to inequity. Several species have shown to respond negatively if they receive a less good reward than another partner for completing the same task. While these experiments show that this behavior is not limited to the human kingdom, they do not provide a functional explanation for the emergence of this behavior. However, emerging data support the hypothesis that an aversion to inequity is a mechanism to promote successful long-term cooperative relationships. In this thesis, a computational model, based on Game Theory, is proposed for analyzing the evolution of cooperation and response to inequity.
