Personalized Free Trials: Design and Evaluation

  • joint work with Hema Yoganarasimhan and Abhishek Pani
A commonly used customer acquisition strategy in the software industry is to offer consumers a limited period to try the product for free. However, no research has empirically examined the issue of optimizing the length of these free trials. Using data from a large-scale field experiment run by a leading software firm, we study the relative effectiveness of three trial lengths -- 7, 14, and 30 days. We find that the shortest trial length of 7 days is the best option at the aggregate level. Next, we employ recently developed machine learning methods for treatment effects to uncover the rich heterogeneity in treatment effects and quantify effects at the individual level. Importantly, we discuss and empirically show that instead of using the standard hyepr-parameter optimization, policy design of optimal treatment assignment policy requires the use of an alternative hyper-parameter optimization method. We then use our estimates to design a personalized targeting policy that maximizes subscriptions and revenues. We show that this optimal policy significantly improves firm's revenues. Finally, we correlate users' optimal free trial length with their pre-treatment and post-treatment variables to offer suggestive evidence on why different trial-length works better for different groups.

Data-driven Discretization of State Space in SingleAgent Dynamic Discrete Choice Models

  • joint work with Hema Yoganarasimhan
Recently, there has been a considerable shift from traditional estimation methods to machine learning enabled methods that makes estimation in high-dimension possible. This trend is boosted by the fact that we are living in a data-abundant era. Companies gather a huge amount of behavioral and contextual data from users to improve the quality of their services. However, machine learning models are more suitable for the prediction task and are not able to estimate counterfactuals. On the other hand, structural models such as single agent dynamic decision model, which traditionally has been used for counterfactual simulations are not high-dimension friendly. In this research, we suggest an algorithm for estimating single agent dynamic decision models that support high-dimensional data settings. We argue that our algorithm has useful properties including consistency of estimates, having linear time complexity with respect to the dimension of independent variables, and being robust to irrelevant independent variables. We test our algorithm by simulating the canonical Rust bus engine replacement problem and show the performance of our algorithm.
